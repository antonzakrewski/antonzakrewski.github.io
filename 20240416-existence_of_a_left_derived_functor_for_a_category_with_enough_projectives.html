existence of a left derived functor for a category with enough projectives

1. Proposition

Let \(\mathcal{A}, \mathcal{B}\) be abelian categories and \(\mathcal{F}: \mathcal{A} \rightarrow \mathcal{B}\) a right exact functor. Suppose \(\mathcal{A}\) admits enough projectives, then theree exists a left derived functor \((\mathrm{LF}_i)\)

2. Proof

2.1. Construction

2.1.1. object

For an object \(A \in \mathcal{A}\) choose a projective resolution

\begin{displaymath} \begin{tikzcd} P_2 \arrow[]{r}{} & P_1 \arrow[]{r}{} & A \arrow[]{r}{} & 0 \end{tikzcd} \end{displaymath}

Then applying \(\mathcal{F}\) – a right exact functor – results in an exact sequence

\begin{displaymath} \begin{tikzcd} \mathcal{F}(P_2) \arrow[]{r}{} & \mathcal{F}(P_1) \arrow[]{r}{} & \mathcal{F}(A) \arrow[]{r}{} & \mathcal{F}(0) \end{tikzcd} \end{displaymath}

Then take the chain complex

\begin{displaymath} \begin{tikzcd} \mathcal{F}(P_2) \arrow[]{r}{} & \mathcal{F}(P_1) \arrow[]{r}{} & \mathcal{F}(0) \end{tikzcd} \end{displaymath}

And define

\begin{align*} \mathcal{LF}_i(A) = H(\mathcal{F}(P_{i+1}) \rightarrow \mathcal{F}(P_i) \rightarrow \mathcal{F}(P_{i-1})) \end{align*}

through the homology functor

2.1.2. morphism

for a morphism

\begin{align*} f: A \rightarrow B \end{align*}

using morphism of objects and projective resolution there exists a diagram

\begin{displaymath} \begin{tikzcd} ... & P_2 \arrow[]{r}{} \arrow[]{d}{} & P_1 \arrow[]{r}{} \arrow[]{d}{} & A \arrow[]{r}{} \arrow[]{d}{} & 0 \arrow[]{d}{} \\ ... & P_2' \arrow[]{r}{} & P_1' \arrow[]{r}{} & B \arrow[]{r}{} & 0 \end{tikzcd} \end{displaymath}

Thus applying \(\mathcal{F}\) and chopping results in

\begin{displaymath} \begin{tikzcd} ... & \mathcal{F}(P_2) \arrow[]{r}{} \arrow[]{d}{} & \mathcal{F}(P_1) \arrow[]{r}{} \arrow[]{d}{} & \mathcal{F}(0) \arrow[]{d}{} \\ ... & \mathcal{F}(P_2') \arrow[]{r}{} & \mathcal{F}(P_1') \arrow[]{r}{} & \mathcal{F}(0) \end{tikzcd} \end{displaymath}

Then let

\begin{align*} \mathcal{LF}_i(f: A \rightarrow B) = H_i(f) \end{align*}

be the morphism induced by the homology functor

fundamental theorem of homological algebra

2.1.3. Proof

2.1.3.1. welldefined on objects

Suppose there exist different projective resolutions. Then they are chain homotopy equivalent (cf. existence of a projective resolution unique up to homotopy for a category admitting enough projectives) Applying \(\mathcal{F}\) preserves the chain homotopy (cf. right exact functor preserves chain homotopies) Using chopping of a chain homotopy, they stay chain homotopic.

2.1.3.2. welldefined on morphisms

Suppose there exist two morphism of objects and projective resolution right exact functor preserves chain homotopies

2.2. delta functor

Let

\begin{displaymath} \begin{tikzcd} 0 \arrow[]{r}{} & A_1 \arrow[]{r}{} & A_2 \arrow[]{r}{} & A_3 \arrow[]{r}{} & 0 \end{tikzcd} \end{displaymath}

be short exact sequence

Then choose projective resolutions

\begin{displaymath} \begin{tikzcd} & P_1^* \arrow[]{d}{} & & P_3^* \arrow[]{d}{} & \\ 0 \arrow[]{r}{} & A_1 \arrow[]{r}{} & A_2 \arrow[]{r}{} & A_3 \arrow[]{r}{} & 0 \end{tikzcd} \end{displaymath}

and use the Horshoe lemma to get

\begin{displaymath} \begin{tikzcd} & P_1^* \arrow[]{r}{} \arrow[]{d}{} & P_1^* \oplus P_3^* \arrow[]{r}{} \arrow[]{d}{} & P_3^* \arrow[]{d}{}& \\ 0 \arrow[]{r}{} & A_1 \arrow[]{r}{} & A_2 \arrow[]{r}{} & A_3 \arrow[]{r}{} & 0 \end{tikzcd} \end{displaymath}

Since the sequence

\begin{displaymath} \begin{tikzcd} & P_1^* \arrow[]{r}{} & P_1^* \oplus P_2^* \arrow[]{r}{} & P_3^* & \\ \end{tikzcd} \end{displaymath}

is degreewise split exact sequence, hence applying the left exact functor preserving split exact sequences it follows that

\begin{displaymath} \begin{tikzcd} 0 \arrow[]{r}{} & \mathcal{F}(P_1^*) \arrow[]{r}{} & \mathcal{F}(P_2^*) \arrow[]{r}{} & \mathcal{F}(P_3^*) \arrow[]{r}{}& 0 \end{tikzcd} \end{displaymath}

is also split exact.

Then using the fundamental theorem of short exact chain complexes there exists a boundary map

\begin{displaymath} \begin{tikzcd} H_n(\mathcal{F}(P_1) \arrow[]{d}{\simeq} & H_{n-1}(\mathcal{F}(P_3) \arrow[]{d}{\simeq} \\ \mathcal{LF}_n(A_3) \arrow[]{r}{} & \mathcal{LF}_{n-1}(A_1) \end{tikzcd} \end{displaymath}

2.2.1. naturality

Consider

\begin{displaymath} \begin{tikzcd} 0 & A_1 & A_2 & A_3 & 0 \\ 0 & B_1 & B_2 & B_3 & 0 \\ \end{tikzcd} \end{displaymath}

choose projctive resolutions of \(f_1: A_1 \rightarrow B_1,f_3: A_3 \rightarrow B_3\) (cf. projective object in the arrow category)

\begin{displaymath} \begin{tikzcd} P_*^1 \arrow[]{r}{} \arrow[]{d}{}& A_1 \arrow[]{d}{}\\ Q_*^1 \arrow[]{r}{} & B_1 \\ \end{tikzcd} \end{displaymath}

and

\begin{displaymath} \begin{tikzcd} P_*^3 \arrow[]{r}{} \arrow[]{d}{}& A_3 \arrow[]{d}{}\\ Q_*^3 \arrow[]{r}{} & B_3 \\ \end{tikzcd} \end{displaymath}

Then again by the horshoe lemma for \(\mathrm{Fun}(\Delta^1, \mathcal{A})\) there exists a projective resolution

\begin{displaymath} \begin{tikzcd} P_*^2 \arrow[]{r}{} \arrow[]{d}{}& A_2 \arrow[]{d}{}\\ Q_*^2 \arrow[]{r}{} & B_2 \\ \end{tikzcd} \end{displaymath}

Therefore there exists a commutative diagram

\begin{displaymath} \begin{tikzcd} 0 \arrow[]{r}{} & P_*^1 \arrow[]{r}{} \arrow[]{d}{} & P_*^2 \arrow[]{r}{} \arrow[]{d}{} & P_*^3 \arrow[]{r}{} \arrow[]{d}{}& 0 \\ 0 \arrow[]{r}{} & Q_*^1 \arrow[]{r}{} & Q_*^2 \arrow[]{r}{} & Q_*^3 \arrow[]{r}{} & 0 \end{tikzcd} \end{displaymath}

with exact rows.

This diagram commutes after applying \(\mathcal{F}\) and hence \(\partial_n\) preserves naturalness by the fundamental theorem of short exact chain complexes

2.3. \(\mathcal{F} \simeq \mathcal{LF}_0\)

By definition

\begin{align*} \mathcal{LF}_0(A) = H_0(\mathcal{F}(P_0)) = \mathrm{coker}( \mathcal{F}(P_1) \rightarrow \mathcal{F}(P_2)) \end{align*}

since \(\mathcal{F}\) is right exact, it preserves cokrnels, hence

\begin{align*} \mathrm{coker}( \mathcal{F}(P_1) \rightarrow \mathcal{F}(P_2)) \simeq& \mathcal{F}(\mathrm{coker}(P_1 \rightarrow P_2)) \\ \sime& \mathcal{F}(A) \end{align*}

2.4. universal delta functor

Let \(A \in \mathcal{A}\). Then choose an epimorphism \(p: P \twoheadrightarrow A\) with \(P\) projective

Since

\begin{displaymath} \begin{tikzcd} ... & 0 \arrow[]{r}{} & P \arrow[]{r}{\mathrm{id}_{P}} & P \end{tikzcd} \end{displaymath}

is a projective resolution, we have

\begin{align*} \mathcal{LF}_n(P) = 0 \end{align*}

for \(n \geq 1\)

Therefore \(\mathcal{LF_n}\) is coeffacable for \(n \geq 1\), as for the short exact sequence

\begin{displaymath} \begin{tikzcd} 0 & \mathrm{ker}(\pi) \arrow[]{r}{}& P \arrow[]{r}{} & A \arrow[]{r}{} & 0 \end{tikzcd} \end{displaymath}

applying \(\mathcal{LF}_n\) for \(n \geq 1\) gives

\begin{displaymath} \begin{tikzcd} 0 \arrow[]{d}{\simeq} \arrow[]{rd}{0}& \\ \mathcal{LF}_n(P) \arrow[]{r}{0}& \mathcal{LF}_n(A) \end{tikzcd} \end{displaymath}

By coeffacable delta functor as universal delta functor \(\{\mathcal{LF}_n\}\) is universal
