space weakly equivalent to a cw-complex

1. Proposition

Let \((X, \mathcal{T})\) be a topological space.

Then there exists a CW-Complex \(A\) and a weak homotopy equivalence \(A \rightarrow X\)

2. Proof

w.l.o.g. let \(X\) be pathconnected.

Otherwise, apply the construction for each path component, then using coproduct of cw complexes as cw complex this is also a cw complex.

chose a basepoint \(x \in X\) an inductively construct maps

\begin{align*} f_n: \mathrm{SK}_n(A) \rightarrow X \end{align*}

from an \(n\)-dimensional CW complex \(\mathrm{SK}_n(A)\) to \(X\) such that

\begin{align*} \pi_k(\mathrm{Sk}_n(A)) \rightarrow \pi_k(X) \end{align*}

is a bijection for \(k < n\). and surjective for \(k = n\)

2.1. inductive start

consider

\begin{align*} \mathrm{SK}_O(A) = \{*\} \rightarrow X \end{align*}

Furthermore, choose a generators \(g_i \in \pi_1(X,x)\) represented by maps \(f_i: S^1 \rightarrow X\) (cf. element of a homotopy group and map out of the sphere)

Then use the wedge product for

\begin{displaymath} \begin{tikzcd} * \arrow[]{r}{} \arrow[]{rd}{} & S^1 \arrow[]{d}{f_i}\\ & X \\ \end{tikzcd} \end{displaymath}

with the induced map

\begin{align*} \bigvee f_i: \bigvee_{i \in I} S^i \rightarrow X \end{align*}

2.2. induction step

Choose generators of the kernel

\begin{align*} f_n: \pi_n(A,a) \rightarrow \pi_n(X,x) \end{align*}

and represent them by maps

\begin{align*} \beta_j: S^n \rightarrow A \end{align*}

Then it follows, that \(f_n \circ \beta_j\) is nullhomotopic hence there exists a homotopy

\begin{align*} H: S^n \times [0,1] \rightarrow X \end{align*}

where

\begin{align*} H[S^n \times \{1\}] \end{align*}

is constant.

Thus there exists the factorization

\begin{displaymath} \begin{tikzcd} D^{n+1} \arrow[]{r}{} \arrow[swap]{rd}{\overline{H}_j} \arrow[]{r}{} & S^{n} \times [0,1] \arrow[]{d}{}\\ & X \end{tikzcd} \end{displaymath}

where

\begin{align*} \pi: D^{n+1} \rightarrow& S^n \times [0,1] \\ (a_0,...,a_n) \mapsto& ([a_0,...,a_{n-1}],a_n) \end{align*}

and the restriction to the boundary is given by

\begin{align*} f_n \beta_j \end{align*}

since

\begin{align*} S^n \times [0,1] / S^n \times \{1\} \simeq D^{n+1} \end{align*}

This results in a diagram

\begin{displaymath} \begin{tikzcd} \coprod_{j \in J} \arrow[]{r}{\coprod \beta_i} \arrow[]{d}{}& \mathrm{sk}_n(A) \arrow[]{d}{f_n} \\ \coprod_{j \in J} D^{n+1} \arrow[]{r}{\coprod \overline{H}_{j}}& X \\ \end{tikzcd} \end{displaymath}

Define \(\mathrm{Sk}_{n+1}(A)\) as the pushout of

\begin{displaymath} \begin{tikzcd} \coprod_{j \in J} \arrow[]{r}{\coprod \beta_i} \arrow[]{d}{}& \mathrm{sk}_n(A) \arrow[]{d}{f_n} \\ \coprod_{j \in J} D^{n+1} \arrow[]{r}{\coprod \overline{H}_{j}}& \mathrm{Sk}_{n+1}(X) \\ \end{tikzcd} \end{displaymath}

using the universal property of the pushout for \(f_n, \overline{H}_n\), we get an extended map

\begin{align*} f_{n+1}: \mathrm{Sk}_{n+1}(A) \rightarrow X \end{align*}

Furhtermore for \(k < n\), there exists the induced group-homomorphisms

\begin{displaymath} \begin{tikzcd} \pi_k(\mathrm{Sk}_n(A),a) \arrow[]{r}{\iota_n} & \pi_k(\mathrm{Sk}_{n+1}(A), a) \arrow[]{r}{f'_{n+1}}& \pi_k(X,x) \end{tikzcd} \end{displaymath}

Here the composition is an isomorphism by induction hypothesis and \(\iota_n\) by isomorphism of homotopy groups between skeleton and CW complex By two out of three for isos, it follows, that

\begin{align*} f'_{n+1} \end{align*}

is also an iso

For \(k = n\), the composition is surjective by induction hypothesis. Hence by precomposition as epimorphism implies epimorphism it follows, that

\begin{align*} f_{n+1}' \end{align*}

is a surjection

By surjection on homotopy groups between skeleton and CW complex, it holds, that

\begin{align*} \iota_n: \pi_k(\mathrm{Sk}_n(A),a) \rightarrow \pi_k(\mathrm{Sk}_{n+1}(A), a) \end{align*}

is a surjection.
