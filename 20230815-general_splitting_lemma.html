splitting Lemma for general groups

1. Proposition

let

\begin{displaymath} \begin{tikzcd} 0 \arrow[]{r}{} & A \arrow[]{r}{\iota} & B \arrow[]{r}{\pi} & C \arrow[]{r}{} & 0 \end{tikzcd} \end{displaymath}

be a short exact sequence of groups

TFAE:

  1. the sequence is split exact sequence
  2. \(\iota\) is a split mono
  3. there exists
\begin{displaymath} \begin{tikzcd} & & B \arrow[]{dd}{\cong} \arrow[]{rd}{}& & \\ 0 \arrow[]{r}{} & A \arrow[]{ru}{} \arrow[]{rd}{} & & C \arrow[]{r}{} & 0 \\ & & A \oplus C \arrow[]{ru}{} & & \\ \end{tikzcd} \end{displaymath}

a natural isomorphism for the direct sum of groups

2. Proof

2.1. 1) \(\implies\) 2)

special case

2.2. 2) \(\implies\) 3)

Define

\begin{align*} \varphi: B \rightarrow& A \oplus C \\ b \mapsto (\iota^{-1}(b), \iota(b)) \end{align*}

It is a group homomorphism, as it is induced by the universal property

\begin{displaymath} \begin{tikzcd} & & A \\ B \arrow[]{rru}{} \arrow[]{rrd}{} \arrow[dashed]{r}{} & A \oplus C \arrow[]{ru}{} \arrow[]{rd}{}& \\ & & C \\ \end{tikzcd} \end{displaymath}

(cf. direct product of groups as categorical product in Grp)

It suffices to show that is bijective (Inverse zu einem Gruppenhomomorphismus für abelsche Gruppen als Gruppenhomomorphismus)

2.2.1. surjective

Let \((a,c') \in A \oplus C\), then by surjectivity of \(\pi: B \rightarrow C\) choose a fibre \(c \in B\). Then consider the element \(\iota(a) \cdot \iota(\iota^{-1}(c)^{-1}) \cdot c\) it follows

\begin{align*} \iota^{-1}(\iota(a) \cdot \iota(\iota^{-1}(c^{-1}) \cdot c) =& \iota^{-1}(\iota(a) \cdot \iota^{-1}(\iota(\iota^{-1}(c)^{-1})) \cdot \iota^{-1}(c) \\ =& a \cdot \iota^{-1}(c)^{-1} \cdot \iota^{-1}(c) \\ =& a \end{align*}

and

\begin{align*} \pi(\iota(a) \cdot \iota(\iota^{-1}(c)^{-1}) \cdot c) =& \pi(\iota(a \cdot \iota^{-1}(c)^{-1})) \cdot \pi(c) \\ =& 1(a \cdot \iota^{-1}(c)^{-1})) \cdot \pi(c) \\ =& 1 \cdot c \\ =& c \end{align*}

2.2.2. injective

  • minimal kernel and monomorphism

Let \(b \in \mathrm{ker}\) then \(b \in \mathrm{ker}(\pi) = \mathrm{im}(\iota)\). hence there exists a fibre \(a\), which is unique as \(\iota\) is injective. as \(0 = \iota^{-1}(b) = \iota^{-1}(\iota(a)) = 0\) it follows that \(0 \in A\) is the unique fibre

2.3. 3) \(\implies\) 1)

follows from the evident maps

\begin{align*} \pi: A \oplus C \rightarrow& A \\ \pi: A \oplus C \rightarrow& C \end{align*}
