irreducible element in general not prime

1. Proposition

Let \(A\) be an Integral domain and \(\alpha \in A\) an irreducible element. Then \(\alpha\) is in general not prime.

2. Proof

Consider \(\mathbb{Z}[ \sqrt{5} \mathrm{i}] \subseteq \mathbb{C}\). It is an Integral domain (cf. field as integral domain, subring of an integral domain)

Then \(\alpha = 3\) is irreducible, but not prime:

Suppose \(\beta \cdot \gamma = 3\), then by multiplicativity of the norm, it follows that

\begin{align*} \| \beta \| \cdot \| \gamma \| = 3 \end{align*}

but

\begin{align*} \| a + \mathrm{i}\sqrt{5} b \| = \sqrt{a^2 + 5b^2} \in \{\sqrt{n} \vert n \in \mathbb{N}_0\} \end{align*}

where the only possibilities are \(\sqrt{3} \cdot \sqrt{3}\) (compare up to \(n = 5\)) or \(\sqrt{9}, 1\). the first case is impossible, as \(a^2,5b^2 \geq 0\) and \(3\) is not a square root and the second implies \(a = \pm 1, b = 0\), hence w.l.o.g. \(\beta\) is a unit

For

\begin{align*} (1 + \mathrm{i} \sqrt{5}) \cdot (1 - \mathrm{i} \sqrt{5}) =& 1 + 5 \\ =& 6 \\ =& 2 \cdot 3 \end{align*}

we conclude, that

\begin{align*} 3 \mid (1 + \mathrm{i} \cdot \sqrt{5}) \cdot (10- \mathrm{i} \sqrt{5}) \end{align*}

but the unique solution (unique inside \(\mathbb{C}\), hence also unique in \(\mathbb{Z}[\mathrm{i} \sqrt{5}]\))

\begin{align*} \frac{1 \pm \mathrm{i} \sqrt{5}}{3} \not\in \mathbb{Z}[\mathrm{i} \sqrt{5}] \end{align*}
