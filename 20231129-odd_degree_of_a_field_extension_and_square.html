odd degree of a field extension and square

1. Proposition

Let \(K,L\) be fields, \(\alpha \in L\) such that the degree \(K(\alpha)\) is odd. Then

\begin{align*} K(\alpha) = K(\alpha^2) \end{align*}

2. Proof

2.1. \(K(\alpha) \supseteq K(\alpha^2)\)

follows from \(\alpha \cdot \alpha \in K(\alpha)\) and definition of \(K(\alpha^2)\) as smallest field extension containing \(\alpha\).

2.2. \(K(\alpha^2) \supseteq K(\alpha)\)

\begin{align*} (\alpha^2)^0, (\alpha^{2})^1, (\alpha^2)^2 ,...,(\alpha^2)^n \end{align*}

are linearly independent. Therefore,

\begin{align*} \mathrm{dim}_{K}(K(x^2)) \geq n + 1 \end{align*}

by Gradsatz, it holds that

\begin{align*} \mathrm{dim}_{K}(K(x)) =& \mathrm{dim}_{K(x^2)}(K(x)) \cdot \mathrm{dim}_{K}(K(x^2)) 2n + 1 =& z \cdot y \end{align*}

with \(y \geq n+1\). Thus, since \(z,y \in \mathbb{N}_0\), the only possibilities are \(z = 1, y = 2n + 1\)
