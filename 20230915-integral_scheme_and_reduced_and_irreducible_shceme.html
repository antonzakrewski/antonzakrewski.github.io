integral scheme and reduced and irreducible scheme

1. Proposition

Let \((X, \mathcal{O}_X, O_i)\) be a scheme

TFAE:

  1. \(X\) is an integral scheme
  2. \(X\) is both reduced and irreducible

2. Proof

2.1. 1) \(\implies\) 2)

2.1.1. reduced

special case, see integral domain as reduced ring

2.1.2. irreducible

Proof by contraposition suppose \(X\) is not irreducible, then there exist disjoint open subsets \(U_1,U_2\). Thus

\begin{align*} \mathcal{O}(U_1 \sqcup U_2) = \mathcal{O}(U_1) \times \mathcal{O}(U_2) \end{align*}

which is not an integral domain, as \((1,0) \cdot (0,1) = (0,0) = 0\)

2.2. 2) \(\implies\) 1)

Suppose \(U \subseteq X\) is open and there exists \(f,g \in \mathcal{O}(U)\) such that

\begin{align*} f \cdot g = 0 \end{align*}

Let

\begin{align*} Y = \{x \in U \vert f_x \in \mathfrak{m}_x\} Z = \{x \in U \vert g_x \in \mathfrak{m}_x\} \end{align*}

Then by <> \(Y,Z\) are closed subsets with

\begin{align*} Y \cup Z = U \end{align*}

but \(X\) is irreducible, and therefore also \(U\) (cf. open set as irreducible sets in an irreducible space).

Thus w.l.o.g. \(Y = U\). But then teh restriction of \(f\) to any open affine subset \(U\) is nilpotent (<>), thus since the scheme is reduced equal to zero.

This shows, that \(X\) is integral.
