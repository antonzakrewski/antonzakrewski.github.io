automorphism group of Z/nZ and unit group

1. Proposition

Let \(n \in \mathbb{N}_0\), then there exists a canonical group-isomorphism

\begin{align*} \mathrm{Aut}(\mathbb{Z}/n \mathbb{Z}) \cong (\mathbb{Z}/ n \mathbb{Z})^{\times} \end{align*}

2. Proof

Note that \(\mathbb{Z}/n \mathbb{Z}\) is a cyclic group generated by \([1]\), hence a group-homomorphism, if welldefined, is determined by \(\varphi(1)\).

Let

\begin{align*} \psi: \mathrm{Aut}(\mathbb{Z}/n \mathbb{Z}) \to& (\mathbb{Z}/ n \mathbb{Z}) \\ \varphi \mapsto& \varphi(1) \\ \varphi_{\alpha} \mapsfrom & \alpha \end{align*}

where

\begin{align*} \varphi_{\alpha}: \mathbb{Z}/n \mathbb{Z} \rightarrow& \mathbb{Z}/ n \mathbb{Z} \\ 1 \mapsto& \alpha \end{align*}

2.1. welldefined

Note that

\begin{align*} \varphi(k \cdot 1) =& \varphi(\sum_{i=1}^{k} 1) \\ =& \sum_{i=1}^{k} \varphi(1) \\ =& k \cdot \varphi(1) \end{align*}

2.1.1. maps to unit

By assumption, \(\varphi \in \mathrm{Aut}( \mathbb{Z}/ n \mathbb{Z})\) is surjective, thus there exists an \(k \in \mathbb{Z}/n \mathbb{Z}\) such that

\begin{align*} \varphi([k]) = [1] \end{align*}

hence

\begin{align*} \varphi([k]) =& \varphi(k \cdot [1]) \\ =& k \cdot \varphi([1]) \\ =& [1] \end{align*}

showing, that \(\varphi([1])\) is a unit

2.1.2. maps to automorphism

2.1.2.1. \(n = 0\)

Then the only units are \(\pm 1\) and hence the maps are

\begin{align*} \varphi: 1 \mapsto& - 1 \\ \varphi: 1 \mapsto& 1 \\ \end{align*}

which are automorphisms

2.1.2.2. else

Then \(\mathbb{Z} / n \mathbb{Z}\) is finite, hence it suffices to show surjectivity (cf. Äquivalenz von Bijektivität, Surjektivität und Injektivität bei gleich mächtigen, endlichen Mengen). Let \([k] \in \mathbb{Z}/n \mathbb{Z}\) be arbitrary and \(l \in \mathbb{Z}/n \mathbb{Z}\) be the inverse of \(\varphi([1])\). Then

\begin{align*} \varphi([k \cdot l \cdot 1]) =& \varphi(k \cdot l \cdot [1]) \\ =& k \cdot l \varphi(1) \\ =& k \cdot [l \cdot \varphi(1)] \\ =& k \cdot [1] \\ =& [k] \end{align*}

2.2. bijective

follows from construction, as the maps are inverse to each other
