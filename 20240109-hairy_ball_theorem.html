hairy ball theorem

1. Proposition

There exists a continuous map

\begin{align*} s: S^n \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^{n+1} \setminus \{0\} \end{align*}

such that \(\langle x, s(x)\rangle = 0\) \(\forall x\) if and only if \(n\) is odd

2. Proof

2.1. \(n\) is odd

By assumption, \(x = (x_1,...,x_{n+1}) \in S^n\). Then the map

\begin{align*} s: S^n \rightarrow& S^n \\ (x_1,...,x_2k) \mapsto& (-x_2,x_1,-x_4,x_3,...,-x_{2k}, x_{2k-1}) \end{align*}

where \(2k = n + 1\)

Hence

\begin{align*} \langle x, s(x)\rangle =& x_1 \cdot - \left(x_2 \right) + x_2 \cdot x_1 ... + x_{2k} \cdot (- x_{2k}) + x_{2k} x_{2k-1} \\ =& 0 \end{align*}

2.2. \(n\) is even

Assume that such an \(s\) exists..

Then

\begin{align*} H: S^n \times [0,1] \rightarrow& S^n \\ (x,t) \mapsto& \cos( \pi t) \cdot x + \sin(\pi t) \frac{s(x)}{\| s(x) \|} \end{align*}

is a homotopy from \(\mathrm{id}_{S^n}\) to \(-\mathrm{id}_{S^n}\). (computation …)

This is a contradicton, to

  1. singular homological degree of the identity on the sphere, \(\mathrm{deg}(\mathrm{id}_{S^{n}}) = 1\)
  2. singular homology degree of the antipodal map, \(\mathrm{deg}(A) = -1\)
  3. singular homological degree invariant under homotopy
