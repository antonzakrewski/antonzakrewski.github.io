numerical polynomial as combination of binomial coefficient polyonmials

1. Proposition

Let \(f: \mathbb{Z} \rightarrow \mathbb{Q}\) be a polynomial and

\begin{align*} \Delta(f): \mathbb{Z} \rightarrow& \mathbb{Q} \\ n \mapsto& f(n+1) - f(n) \end{align*}

TFAE:

  1. \(f\) is a $\mathbb{Z}$-linear combination of binomial coefficient polynomial, \(Q_i(x)\)
  2. \(f\) is a numerical polynomial
  3. \(f(n) \in \mathbb{Z}\) for almost all \(n \in \mathbb{N}\)
  4. \(\Delta f\) is a $\mathbb{Z}$-linear combination of binomial coefficient polynomials and there exists an \(n \in \mathbb{N}\) with \(f(n) \in \mathbb{Z}\)

2. Proof

2.1. 1) \(\implies\) 2)

follows from:

  • combinatorial meaning of the binomial coefficient
  • integers as ring

2.2. 2) \(\implies\) 3)

special case

2.3. 1) \(\implies\) 4)

For each \(Q_l = \binom{n}{l}\), it holds, that

\begin{align*} \Delta (Q_l)(n) =& Q_l(n + 1) - Q_l(n) \\ =& \binom{n+1}{l} - \binom{n}{l} \end{align*}

and by recursion formula, we conclude

\begin{align*} \binom{n+1}{l} - \binom{n}{l} =& \binom{n}{l-1} \\ =& Q_{l-1}(n) \end{align*}

Hence

\begin{align*} \Delta Q_l = Q_{l-1} \end{align*}

2.4. 4) \(\implies\) 1)

Let

\begin{align*} \Delta f =& \sum m_i Q_i(x) \end{align*}

Let

\begin{align*} g = \sum m_i Q_{i+1}(x) \end{align*}

Then by previous remark, we get

\begin{align*} \Delta g = \Delta f \end{align*}

and therefore, \(f - g\) is constant. Thus

\begin{align*} f = g + m && m \in \mathbb{Q} \end{align*}

Choose \(n \in \mathbb{N}\) with \(f(n) \in \mathbb{Z}\), then we get

\begin{align*} f(n) \in \mathbb{Z} \\ g(n) \in \mathbb{Z} \\ f(n) - g(n) = m \in \mathbb{Z} \end{align*}

2.5. 3) \(\implies\) 4)

the second part is a special case,

for the first part, Proof by induction for \(\mathrm{deg}(f)\)

2.5.1. base case

Let \(\mathrm{deg}(f) \leq 0\), then \(f\) is constant, therefore, \(\Delta f = 0\).

2.5.2. induction step

By assumption, \(\Delta f(n) = f(n+1) - f(n) \in \mathbb{Z}\) for almost all \(n \in \mathbb{Z}\). Therefore, by induction hypothesis, \(\Delta \Delta f\) is a \(\mathbb{Z}\)-linear combination.

Thus by 4) \(\implies\) 1) for \(\Delta \Delta f\), we conclude, that \(\Delta f\) is a \(\mathbb{Z}\)-linear combination
