prime in a localization in general not prime before

1. Proposition

Let \(A\) be a commutative ring, \(S \subseteq A\) a multiplicatively closed subset, \(\alpha \in A\) such that

\begin{align*} \frac{a}{1} \in S^{-1}A \end{align*}

is prime (resp. irreducible)

Then \(\alpha \in A\) is in general not prime (resp. irreducible)

2. Proof

Consider \(A = \mathbb{Z}\) and \(S = \{2^n\}\). Then for \(\frac{6}{1} \in S \mathbb{Z}\), it holds that \(\frac{6}{1} \sim \frac{3}{1}\) for multiplication with \(\frac{1}{2}, 2\).

Hence by associated and prime it is equivalent to show, that \(\frac{3}{1}\) is prime (irreducible) in \(S \mathbb{Z}\)

This follows, since \(3 \in \mathbb{Z}\) is prime and by localization preserving prime as \(\frac{3}{1}\) is not a unit, since \(\frac{1}{3} \not\in S\).

Nonetheless, \(6 = 3 \cdot 2\) is not prime in \(\mathbb{Z}\) and since the \(\mathbb{Z}\) are an UFD hence also not irreducible (cf. irreducible and prime element in an UFD)
