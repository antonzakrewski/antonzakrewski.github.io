invariance of the dimension of the euclidean space

1. Proposition

Suppose

\begin{align*} \mathbb{R}^n \cong \mathbb{R}^m \end{align*}

are homeomorphic

Then \(n = m\)

2. Proof

2.1. \(n \neq 1\)

By restriction of a homeomorphism as homeomorphism, we get homeomorphisms

\begin{align*} \mathbb{R}^n \setminus \{0\} \cong \mathbb{R}^m \setminus \{f(0)\} \cong \mathbb{R}^m \setminus \{0\} \end{align*}

By euclidean space without a point homotopy equivalent to a sphere, we get

\begin{align*} S^{n-1} \cong \mathbb{R}^n \setminus \{0\} \cong \mathbb{R}^m \setminus \{f(0)\} \cong S^{m-1} \end{align*}

Since singular homology functor is a homotopy invariant functor, we get

\begin{align*} \mathbb{Z} \cong H_{n-1}(S^{n-1}) \cong H_{n-1}(S^{m-1}) \end{align*}

where for \(n \neq 1, m \neq n\) it follows that \(H_{n-1}(S^{m-1}) \cong 0\), hence contradiction.

2.2. \(n = 1\)

Note that we get a homeomorphism

\begin{align*} \mathbb{R} \setminus \{0\} \cong \mathbb{R}^m \setminus \{f(0)\} \end{align*}

Here for \(m \geq 2\), applying the path component functor, we get

\begin{align*} \pi_0(\mathbb{R}^m \setminus \{f(0)\}) = \{*\} \end{align*}

and

\begin{align*} \pi_0(\mathbb{R} \setminus \{0\}) = \{1,2\} \end{align*}

hence \(m = 1\)
