Lazard's theorem

1. Proposition

Let \(A\) be a ring and \(M\) be \(A\)-Modul

TFAE:

  1. \(M\) is a filtered colimit of finitely generated free modules
  2. \(M\) is a filtered colimit of free modules
  3. \(M\) is a filtered colimit of projective module
  4. \(M\) is a filtered colimit of flat modules
  5. \(M\) is flat module

2. Proof

2.1. 1) \(\implies\) 2)

special case

2.2. 2) \(\implies\) 3)

corollary of free module as projective module

2.3. 3) \(\implies\) 4)

corollary of projective module as flat module

2.4. 4) \(\implies\) 5)

corollary of filtered colimit of flat modules as flat module

2.5. 5) \(\implies\) 1)

Let \(E(M)\) be the coslice category of finitely generated free modules. Then by module as colimit of the coslice category of finitely generated modules it remains to show that this subcategory is filtered

2.5.1. existence of a morphism

follows from

\begin{displaymath} \begin{tikzcd} R^k \arrow[]{d}{\iota_1} \arrow[bend left]{rd}{\alpha}& \\ R^k \oplus R^l \arrow[]{r}{(\alpha \oplus \beta)} & M \\ R^l \arrow[]{u}{\iota_2} \arrow[bend right]{ru}{\beta} & \\ \end{tikzcd} \end{displaymath}

2.5.2. cofork

Suppose there exists parallel morphism, i.e.

\begin{displaymath} \begin{tikzcd} R^k \arrow[]{dd}{\mathrm{id}} \arrow[]{rd}{f} \arrow[bend left]{rrd}{\gamma} & & \\ & R^l \arrow[]{r}{\alpha} & M \\ R^k \arrow[]{ru}{g} \arrow[bend right]{rru}{\gamma} & & \end{tikzcd} \end{displaymath}

where

\begin{align*} \gamma =& \alpha \circ f \\ \gamma =& \alpha \circ g \end{align*}

then there exists some

\begin{align*} h: R^l \rightarrow R^n \end{align*}

with

\begin{displaymath} \begin{tikzcd} R^l \arrow[]{dd}{h} \arrow[]{rd}{\alpha}& \\ & M \\ R^n \arrow[]{ru}{\beta} & \\ \end{tikzcd} \end{displaymath}

such that

\begin{align*} h \circ f = h \circ g \end{align*}

By replacing \(f\) with \(f - g\) we may assume w.l.o.g. that \(g = 0\) ???

By induction on \(k\) we may reduce the case to \(k = 1\) ???

Then \(f\) corresponds to an element in \(R^l\), i.e. a tuple \((r_1,...,r_l)\) and \(\alpha\) to an element \((m_1,...,m_l)\) (cf. hom out of a ring)

Furthermore the assumption that

\begin{align*} 0 =& \alpha \circ g \\ =& \gamma \\ =& \alpha \circ f \end{align*}

shows that

\begin{align*} 0 =& (\alpha \circ f)(1) \\ =& \sum r_i m_i \\ \end{align*}

Let \(I \coloneqq (r_1,...,r_l) \subseteq R\) be the right ideal generated by those elements.

Since \(M\) is flat, it preserves injectivity of the submodule inclusion

\begin{align*} I \hookrightarrow R \end{align*}

showing that

\begin{align*} \iota': I \otimes_R M \hookrightarrow R \otimes_R M \simeq M \end{align*}

is injective

in particular this shows

\begin{align*} \sum_{i=1}^l (r_i \otimes m_i) =& 0 \in I \otimes_R M \end{align*}

Consider the exact sequence

\begin{displaymath} \begin{tikzcd} 0 \arrow[]{r}{} & \mathrm{ker} \arrow[]{r}{} & R^l \arrow[twoheadrightarrow, ]{r}{\pi}& I \arrow[]{r}{}& 0 \end{tikzcd} \end{displaymath}

where

\begin{align*} R^l \rightarrow& I \\ e_i \mapsto& r_i \end{align*}

Then by flatness the sequence

\begin{displaymath} \begin{tikzcd} 0 \arrow[]{r}{} & \mathrm{ker} \otimes_R M \arrow[]{r}{} & R^l \otimes_R M \arrow[twoheadrightarrow, ]{r}{\pi}& I \otimes_R M \arrow[]{r}{}& 0 \end{tikzcd} \end{displaymath}

is exact

This shows that

\begin{align*} \sum_{i=1}^l e_i \otimes m_i \in \mathrm{ker} \otimes_R M \end{align*}

since \(e_i \otimes_R m_i \mapsto r_i \otimes_R m_i\)
