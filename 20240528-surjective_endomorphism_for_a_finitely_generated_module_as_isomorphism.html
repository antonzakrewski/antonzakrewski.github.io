surjective endomorphism for a finitely generated module as isomorphism

1. Proposition

Let \(A\) be a commutative ring, \(M\) a finitely generated module and \(f: M \rightarrow M\) a algebraic module-epimorphism Then \(f\) is an module-isomorphism

2. Proof

consider \(M\) as module over the polynomial ring \(A[X]\) (cf. module with an endomorphism as module over the polynomial ring)

Then \(M\) is a finitely generated \(A[X]\) module (cf. generator of a module over a subring as generator over the ring)

Let \(\mathfrak{a} = (X) \subseteq A[X]\). Then

\begin{align*} \mathfrak{a}M = A[X] X \cdot M \end{align*}

where by surjectivity of \(f\) we conclude that

\begin{align*} \mathfrak{a} M =& f[M] \\ =& M \end{align*}

By general Nakayama's lemma there exists a polynomial \(\sum \alpha_i X^i \in (X) \subseteq A[X]\) such that

\begin{align*} (1 - \sum \alpha_i X^i )(M) = 0 \end{align*}

Let \(m \in M\) with \(f(m) = 0\). Then we get

\begin{align*} 0 =& (1 - \sum \alpha_i X^i)(m) \\ =& (\mathrm{id} - \sum \alpha_i X^i)(m) \\ =& (\mathrm{id}(m) - \sum \alpha_i X^i(m)) \\ =& m - \sum \alpha_i 0 \\ =& m \end{align*}

thus we are done by minimal kernel and monomorphism

Note that

\begin{align*} \sum \alpha_i f^{i-1} \end{align*}

gives an inverse of \(f\)
