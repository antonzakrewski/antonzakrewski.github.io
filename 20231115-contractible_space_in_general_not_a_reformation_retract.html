contractible space in general not a deformation retract

1. Proposition

Let \((X, \mathcal{T})\) be a topological space, that is contractible. Then \(X\) is in general not deformation retract

2. Proof

Consider

\begin{align*} S = \{(x,y) \in \mathbb{R}^2 \vert \frac{y}{x} \in \mathbb{Q}\} \end{align*}

Then \(S\) is contractible, but not a deformation retract for \((1,0)\)

2.1. contractible

The map

\begin{align*} \varphi: [0,1] \times S \rightarrow S \\ (t,x) \mapsto x - t \cdot x \end{align*}

is a contraction to \(0\) and then concatenation with

\begin{align*} \psi: [0,1] \times S \rightarrow& S \\ s \mapsto& (t,0) \end{align*}

is a contraction to \((1,0)\)

2.2. not deformation retract

but choosing the epsilon-neighbourhood of \((1,0)\) with radius \(0.5\)

\begin{align*} B_{0.5}(1,0) \end{align*}

is not a path connected space.

Threfore contradiction to deformation retract as locally nullhomotopic
