Kaplansky Characterization of an UFD

1. Proposition

Let \(R\) be an Integral domain.

TFAE:

  1. \(R\) is an UFD
  2. every nonzero prime ideal \(\mathfrak{p} \subseteq R\) contains a prime element

2. Proof

2.1. 1) \(\implies\) 2)

Let \(\mathfrak{p}\) be a nonzero prime ideal, then there exists an \(r \in \mathfrak{p}\) with \(r \neq 0\). By assumption, there exists a prime decomposition

\begin{align*} r = \alpha \cdot p_1 \cdot ... \cdot p_k \end{align*}

Since \(\mathfrak{p}\) is strict, it follows, that \(r\) is not a unit and hence \(k \geq 1\). Furthermore, because \(\mathfrak{p}\) is prime, we conclude by induction, that \(p_j \in \mathfrak{p}\) for some \(j \in \{1,...,k\}\), since

\begin{align*} \alpha^{-1} \cdot (\alpha \cdot (p_1 \cdot ... \cdot p_k)) = (p_1 \cdot ... \cdot p_k) \in \mathfrak{p} \end{align*}

and

\begin{align*} p_1 \cdot (p_2 \cdot ... \cdot p_k) \in \mathfrak{p} \Rightarrow \begin{cases} p_1 \in \mathfrak{p}& (p_2 \cdot ... \cdot p_k) \in \mathfrak{p} \end{cases} \end{align*}

2.2. 2) \(\implies\) 1)invert

Proof by contraposition Suppose \(R\) is not an UFD, then by ufd and units and prime products there exists an \(x \not\in T = R^{\times} \cup \{p_1 \cdot ... p_n\}\) and \(x \neq 0\) (cf. invertible and prime product set)

Then, as \(T\) is prime-ish it follows, that

\begin{align*} (x) \cap T = \emptyset \end{align*}

as otherwise, there would exist an \(r \in R\) such that

\begin{align*} r \cdot x \in T \end{align*}

and hence \(x \in T\), contradiction.
