Proof by contradiction

Let \(a \in A\) such that \(a\) is not nilpotent. Then by the Pigeonhole principle, there exists an \(n \in \mathbb{N}\) such that

\begin{align*} a^n = a^{n+1} \neq 0 \end{align*}

We may assume w.l.o.g., that \(1 - a^{n}, 1 + a^n\) are units, because otherwise there exists an maximal ideal \(\mathfrak{m} \supseteq (1 - a^{n})\) with \(a \not \in \mathfrak{m}\) because of \(1 - a^n + a \cdot a^{n-1} = 1\) (see: maximal ideal and non-unit Then we get

\begin{align*} (1 - a^{n}) (1 + a^n) =& 1 - a^{2n} \\ =& 1 - a^n \end{align*}

and hence also (cf. neutral element and neutral element for a unit)

\begin{align*} 1 - a^n =& 1 \\ a^n =& 0 \end{align*}

contradiction
