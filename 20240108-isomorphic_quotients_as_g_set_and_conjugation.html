isomorphic quotients as G-Set and conjugation

1. Proposition

Let \(G\) be a group, \(G/H\) and \(G/K\) be G-Sets for subgroups \(H,K \subseteq G\)

TFAE:

  1. \(G/H\) and \(G/K\) are isomorphic as G-Sets
  2. there exists an \(g \in G\) such that
\begin{align*} g \circ H g^{-1} = K \end{align*}

2. Proof

2.1. 1) \(\implies\) 2)

Choose a \(g \in G\) with

\begin{align*} \varphi([1]) = [g] \end{align*}

Then for arbitrary \(g' \in G\), it follows, that

\begin{align*} \varphi([g']) =& \varphi([g' \cdot 1]) \\ =&\varphi(g' \cdot [1]) \\ =& g' \cdot \varphi([1]) \\ =& g' \cdot [g] \\ =& [g' \cdot g] \end{align*}

Then it follows, for \(h \in H\), that

\begin{align*} [g^{-1} h \cdot g] =& \varphi[g^{-1} h] \\ =& \varphi[g^{-1}] \\ =& [1] \end{align*}

hence there exists an \(k \in K\) with \(g^{-1} h g = k\) resp.

\begin{align*} g^{-1} H g \subseteq K \end{align*}

Note that by analogous arguments,

\begin{align*} \varphi^{-1}[x] =& \varphi^{-1}((x \cdot g^{-1}) \cdot [g]) \\ =& (x \cdot g^{-1}) \cdot \varphi^{-1}([g]) \\ =& (x \cdot g^{-1}) \cdot [1] \\ =& [x \cdot g^{-1}] \end{align*}

and by similar arguments, it follows, that

\begin{align*} g K g^{-1} \subseteq H \end{align*}

Therefore, conjugation with \(g\) resp. \(g^{-1}\) are welldefined maps, which are inverse to each other

2.2. 2) \(\implies\) 1)

let

\begin{align*} \varphi: G/H \rightarrow& G/K \\ [x] \mapsto& [x g^{-1}] \end{align*}

2.2.1. g-equivariant

follows from

\begin{align*} \varphi(g' \cdot [x]) =& \varphi([g' \cdot x]) \\ =& [g' \cdot x \cdot g^{-1}] \\ =& g' \cdot [x \cdot g^{-1}] \\ =& g' \cdot \varphi([x]) \end{align*}

2.2.2. welldefined

Let \([x] = [x']\), then there exists an \(h \in H\) such that

\begin{align*} x = x' \cdot h \end{align*}

thus consider

\begin{align*} \varphi([x]) =& \varphi([x' \cdot h]) \\ =& [x' \cdot h \cdot g^{-1}] \\ =& [x' \cdot g^{-1} \cdot g h \cdot g^{-1}] \\ =& [x' \cdot g^{-1} \cdot k] \\ =& [x' \cdot g^{-1}] \end{align*}

2.2.3. injective

Suppose that there exists \(x,y \in G\) such that

\begin{align*} \varphi([x]) =& \varphi([y]) \\ [x \cdot g^{-1}] =& [y \cdot g^{-1}] \end{align*}

Then there exists an \(k \in K\) such that

\begin{align*} x \cdot g^{-1} = y \cdot g^{-1} k \end{align*}

Since conjugation is an isomorphism, there exists an \(h \in H\) such that

\begin{align*} g \cdot h \cdot g^{-1} = k \end{align*}

and thus

\begin{align*} x \cdot g^{-1} =& y \cdot g^{-1} g \cdot h \cdot g^{-1} \\ x \cdot g^{-1} =& y \cdot h \cdot g^{-1} \\ x =& y \cdot h \end{align*}

Thus showing, that

\begin{align*} [x] = [y] \in G/H \end{align*}

2.2.4. surjective

follows from \([x \cdot g]\)
