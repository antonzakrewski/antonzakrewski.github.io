contravariant co-Yoneda Lemma

1. Proposition

Let \(\mathcal{C}\) be a small category and \(\mathcal{F}: \mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}} \rightarrow \mathrm{Set}\) a set-valued presheaf Then there exists a canonical isomorphism

\begin{align*} \varphi: \mathrm{colim}_{X \in \mathcal{C}/\mathcal{F}} \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(-, X) \rightarrow \mathcal{F}(-) \end{align*}

where \(\mathcal{C}/\mathcal{F}\) is the slice category by yoneda

2. Proof

Abuse of notation. The natural transformation of \(a \in \mathcal{F}(A)\) is simply written as \(a\)

By assumption we have a diagram of the form for \(f: B \rightarrow A\)

\begin{displaymath} \begin{tikzcd} (\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(-,A),a) \arrow[]{rr}{f^{\leftarrow}}& & (\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(-,B),b)\\ \end{tikzcd} \end{displaymath}

for \(a \in \mathcal{F}(A), b \in \mathcal{F}(B)\) such that

\begin{align*} \mathcal{F}(f)(a) = b \end{align*}

Then it follows that

\begin{displaymath} \begin{tikzcd} (\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(-,A),a) \arrow[]{rd}{a} \arrow[]{rr}{f^{\leftarrow}}& & (\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(-,B),b) \arrow[swap]{ld}{b} \\ & \mathcal{F}(-) & \end{tikzcd} \end{displaymath}

commutes, as by yoneda

\begin{align*} f^{ \leftarrow}(a) = b \end{align*}

Suppose there exists a presheaf \(\mathcal{G}: \mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}} \rightarrow \mathrm{Set}\)

\begin{displaymath} \begin{tikzcd} (\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(-,A),a) \arrow[swap, bend right]{rdd}{a_{\mathcal{G}}} \arrow[]{rd}{a} \arrow[]{rr}{f^{\leftarrow}}& & (\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(-,B),b) \arrow[swap]{ld}{b} \arrow[bend left]{ldd}{b_{\mathcal{G}}}\\ & \mathcal{F}(-) & \\ & \mathcal{G}(-) & \end{tikzcd} \end{displaymath}

Then we define

\begin{align*} \eta: \mathcal{F}(A) \rightarrow& \mathcal{G}(A) \\ a \mapsto& a_{\mathcal{G}} \end{align*}

2.1. naturality

Let \(a \in \mathcal{F}(A)\) and \(f: B \rightarrow A\) a morphism between \((A,a) = (A, \mathcal{F}(f)(b)) \rightarrow (B,b)\)

Then it remains to show that

\begin{displaymath} \begin{tikzcd} \mathcal{F}(A) \arrow[]{d}{\tau(A)} \arrow[]{r}{\mathcal{F}(f)} & \mathcal{F}(B) \arrow[]{d}{\tau(B)}\\ \mathcal{G}(A) \arrow[]{r}{\mathcal{G}(f)} & \mathcal{G}(B) \\ \end{tikzcd} \end{displaymath}

commutes.

Then it follows that

\begin{displaymath} \begin{tikzcd} \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(-,A) \arrow[]{d}{} \arrow[bend right]{dd}{}\\ \mathcal{F}(-) \arrow[]{d}{\tau} \\ \mathcal{G}(-) \end{tikzcd} \end{displaymath}

commutes elementwise for \(- = A\)

Consider

\begin{displaymath} \begin{tikzcd} \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(A,A) \arrow[]{d}{a} \arrow[bend right, swap]{dd}{a_{\mathcal{G}}} \arrow[]{r}{f^{ \rightarrow}} & \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(B,A) \arrow[swap]{d}{a} \arrow[bend left]{dd}{a_\mathcal{G}} \\ \mathcal{F}(A) \arrow[]{d}{\tau(A)} \arrow[]{r}{\mathcal{F}(f)} & \mathcal{F}(B) \arrow[swap]{d}{\tau(B)} \\ \mathcal{G}(A) \arrow[]{r}{\mathcal{G}(f)} & \mathcal{G}(B) \\ \end{tikzcd} \end{displaymath}

Here it suffices to show that the outer square

\begin{displaymath} \begin{tikzcd} \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(A,A) \arrow[bend right, swap]{d}{a_{\mathcal{G}}} \arrow[]{r}{f^{ \rightarrow}} & \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(B,A) \arrow[bend left]{d}{a_\mathcal{G}} \\ \mathcal{G}(A) \arrow[]{r}{\mathcal{G}(f)} & \mathcal{G}(B) \\ \end{tikzcd} \end{displaymath}

commutes, as

\begin{align*} a(\mathrm{id}_{A}) =& \mathrm{id}_{\mathcal{F}(A)}(a) \\ =& a \end{align*}

and \(a \in \mathcal{F}(A)\) was choosen arbitrary.

This follows by naturality of the yoneda lemma

2.2. uniqueness

Suppose there exists a \(\tau: \mathcal{F}(-) \rightarrow \mathcal{G}(-)\). Then for arbitrary \(a \in \mathcal{F}(A)\) it follows that

\begin{displaymath} \begin{tikzcd} (\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(A,A),a) \arrow[swap, bend right]{rdd}{a_{\mathcal{G}}} \arrow[]{rd}{a} & \\ & \mathcal{F}(A) \arrow[]{d}{\tau(A)} \\ & \mathcal{G}(A) \end{tikzcd} \end{displaymath}

commutes.

By yoneda it follows that

\begin{align*} \tau(A)(a) =& \tau(A)(\mathrm{id}_{\mathcal{F}(A)}(a)) \\ =& \tau(A)(\mathcal{F}(\mathrm{id}_{A})(a)) \\ =& \tau(A)(a(\mathrm{id})) \\ =& a_{\mathcal{G}}(\mathrm{id}) \\ =& \mathcal{G}(\mathrm{id})(a_{\mathcal{G}}) \\ =& \mathrm{id}_{\mathcal{G}(A)}(a_{\mathcal{G}}) \\ =& a_{\mathcal{G}} \end{align*}

Hence \(\tau(A)\) maps \(a \in \mathcal{F}(A)\) uniquely. Thus \(\tau\) is unique.
