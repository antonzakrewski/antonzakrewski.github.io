coeffacable delta functor as universal delta functor

1. Proposition

Let \(\mathcal{A}, \mathcal{B}\) be abelian categories and \(\mathcal{F}_n: \mathcal{A} \rightarrow \mathcal{B}\) a delta functor. suppose that \(\mathcal{F}_n\) is coeffacable for \(n \geq 1\), then \((\mathcal{F}_n)\) is a universal delta functor

2. Proof

2.1. uniqueness of a morphism

Let \(\mathcal{G}_n\) be another delta functor and \(\tau: \{\mathcal{G}_n\} \rightarrow \{\mathcal{F}_n\}\) be a morphism of delta functors. Let \(A \in \mathcal{A}\) and pick an epimorphism \(p: P \rightarrow A\) such that \(\mathcal{F}_n(p) = 0\)

Then we get a short exact sequence

\begin{displaymath} \begin{tikzcd} 0 & \mathrm{ker}(p) & P & A & 0 \end{tikzcd} \end{displaymath}

(see: exact sequence and epimorphisms)

Thus we get

\begin{displaymath} \begin{tikzcd} \mathcal{G}_n(P) \arrow[]{r}{\mathcal{G}(p)} \arrow[]{d}{\tau_n(P)} & \mathcal{G}_n(A) \arrow[]{d}{\tau_{n}(A)} \arrow[]{r}{\partial_n} & \mathcal{G}_{n-1}(\mathrm{ker}(p)) \arrow[]{d}{\tau_{n-1}(\mathrm{ker}(p))} \\ \mathcal{F}_n(P) \arrow[]{r}{0} & \mathcal{F}_n(A) \arrow[rightarrowtail]{r}{\partial_n} & \mathcal{F}_{n-1}(\mathrm{ker}(p)) \end{tikzcd} \end{displaymath}

Then by exactness (since \(\mathrm{im}(0) = 0\)) it follows, that \(\partial_n\) is a monomorphism. Therefore \(\tau_n(A)\) is uniquely (cf. postcomposition with a morphism as injection on hom sets and monomorphism) determined by

\begin{align*} \partial_n \circ \tau_n(A) = \tau_{n-1}(\mathrm{ker}(p)) \circ \partial_n \end{align*}

where by induction \(\tau_{n-1}\) is already given and \(\partial_n\) all the more.

Hence if there exists an an extension \(\tau_n\) of \(\tau_0\), it is unique.

2.2. existence of a morphism

Let \(\tau_{0}: \mathcal{G}_0 \rightarrow \mathcal{F}_0\) be a natural transformation. Then we construct \(\tau\) be induction, so assume \(\tau_{n-1}\) is given. Then for \(A \in \mathrm{Ob}(\mathcal{A})\) choose an epi \(p: P \twoheadrightarrow A\) such that \(\mathcal{F}_n(p) = 0\).

Then we get

\begin{displaymath} \begin{tikzcd} & \mathcal{G}_n(A) \arrow[]{r}{\partial} \arrow[]{rd}{g} \arrow[bend left]{rr}{0}& \mathcal{G}_{n-1}(\mathrm{ker}(p)) \arrow[]{r}{} \arrow[]{d}{} & \arrow[]{d}{} \mathcal{G}_{n-1}(P) \\ 0 \arrow[]{r}{} & \mathcal{F}_n(A) \arrow[rightarrowtail]{r}{\partial} & \mathcal{F}_{n-1}(\mathrm{ker}(p)) \arrow[]{r}{} & \mathcal{F}_{n-1}(P) \end{tikzcd} \end{displaymath}

where \(\mathcal{F}(\iota) \circ g = 0\). Therefore there exists a morphism

\begin{displaymath} \begin{tikzcd} \mathcal{G}_n(A) \arrow[dashed]{d}{}\arrow[swap, dashed]{rrd}{\exists!} \arrow[bend left]{rrrd}{g} \arrow[bend left]{rrrrd}{0}& & & & \\ \mathcal{F}_n(A) \arrow[]{r}{\simeq} & \mathrm{im}(\partial) \arrow[]{r}{\simeq} & \mathrm{ker}(\mathcal{F}(\iota)) \arrow[]{r}{} & \mathcal{F}_{n-1}(\mathrm{ker}(p)) \arrow[]{r}{\mathcal{F}(\iota)} & \mathcal{F}_{n-1}(P) \\ \end{tikzcd} \end{displaymath}
