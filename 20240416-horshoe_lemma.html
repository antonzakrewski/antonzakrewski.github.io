Horshoe lemma

1. Proposition

Let \(\mathcal{A}\) be an abelian category and

\begin{displaymath} \begin{tikzcd} 0 \arrow[]{r}{} & A_1 \arrow[]{r}{} & A_2 \arrow[]{r}{} & A_3 \arrow[]{r}{} & 0 \end{tikzcd} \end{displaymath}

a short exact sequence Suppose \(P_{*}^1 \rightarrow A_1, P_{*}^{3} \rightarrow A_3\) are projective resolutions.

Then there exists a projective resolution \(P_{*}^2 \rightarrow A_2\) and maps such that

\begin{displaymath} \begin{tikzcd} & \vdots \arrow[]{d}{} & \vdots \arrow[]{d}{}& \vdots \arrow[]{d}{} & \\ 0 \arrow[]{r}{} & P_3^1 \arrow[]{d}{} \arrow[dashed]{r}{} & P_3^2 \arrow[]{d}{} \arrow[dashed]{r}{}& P_3^3 \arrow[]{d}{} \arrow[]{r}{} & 0\\ 0 \arrow[]{r}{} & P_2^1 \arrow[]{d}{} \arrow[dashed]{r}{} & P_2^2 \arrow[]{d}{} \arrow[dashed]{r}{}& P_2^3 \arrow[]{d}{} \arrow[]{r}{} & 0\\ 0 \arrow[]{r}{} & P_1^1 \arrow[]{d}{} \arrow[dashed]{r}{} & P_1^2 \arrow[]{d}{} \arrow[dashed]{r}{}& P_1^3 \arrow[]{d}{} \arrow[]{r}{} & 0 \\ 0 \arrow[]{r}{} & \arrow[]{r}{} A_1 \arrow[]{r}{}& A_2 \arrow[]{r}{}& A_3 \arrow[]{r}{} & 0 \end{tikzcd} \end{displaymath}

such that each row

\begin{displaymath} \begin{tikzcd} 0 \arrow[]{r}{} & P_n^1 \arrow[]{r}{} & P_n^2 \arrow[]{r}{} & P_2^3 \arrow[]{r}{} & 0 \end{tikzcd} \end{displaymath}

is split exact

2. Proof

sloppy at times

2.1. construction

We construct such a resolution by induction: Let

\begin{align*} P_n^2 = P_n^3 \oplus P_n^1 \end{align*}

With the morphisms

\begin{displaymath} \begin{tikzcd} P_n^1 \arrow[rightarrowtail]{r}{\iota}& P_n^1 \oplus P_n^3 \arrow[]{r}{\pi}& P_n^3 \end{tikzcd} \end{displaymath}

This is clearly split exact (cf. Splitting Lemma)

Then it remains to construct a differential

\begin{align*} \pi: P_{n}^1 \oplus P_{n}^3 \rightarrow& P_{n-1}^1 \oplus P_{n-1}^3 \end{align*}

with the convention \(P_{0}^i = A_i\)

Since \(P_n^3\) is projective, there exists a lift

\begin{displaymath} \begin{tikzcd} & P_{n}^3 \arrow[dashed]{ld}{\exists q_{n}^3}} \arrow[]{d}{} \\ P_{n-1}^2 \arrow[twoheadrightarrow]{r}{} & P_{n-1}^{3} \\ \end{tikzcd} \end{displaymath}

Then we define

\begin{align*} p_n^2 \coloneqq \iota_{n-1} p_{n}^1 + q_{n}^3 \end{align*} \begin{displaymath} \begin{tikzcd} & P_{n}^1 \arrow[]{r}{} \arrow[]{d}{p_{n}^1} & P_{n}^1 \oplus P_n^3 \arrow[]{r}{} \arrow[swap]{d}{\iota_{n-1} p_n^1 + q_n^3}& P_{n}^3 \arrow[dashed]{ld}{}& \\ 0 \arrow[]{r}{} & P_{n-1}^1 \arrow[]{r}{\iota} & P_{n-1}^2 \arrow[]{r}{} & P_n^3 \arrow[]{r}{} & 0 \end{tikzcd} \end{displaymath}

2.2. commutes

Then it follows that

\begin{displaymath} \begin{tikzcd} P_n^1 \arrow[]{r}{} \arrow[]{d}{} & P_{n}^2 \arrow[]{d}{} \\ P_{n-1}^1 \arrow[]{r}{}& P_{n-1}^2 \\ \end{tikzcd} \end{displaymath}

and

\begin{displaymath} \begin{tikzcd} P_n^2 \arrow[]{r}{} \arrow[]{d}{} & P_{n}^3 \arrow[]{d}{} \\ P_{n-1}^2 \arrow[]{r}{}& P_{n-1}^3 \\ \end{tikzcd} \end{displaymath}

commute as

\begin{align*} \iota(p_n^2) =& \iota_{n-1} p_n^1 + q_n^3 \circ 0\\ =& \iota_{n-1} p_n^1 \end{align*}

and

\begin{align*} \pi(p_n^2) =& \pi(\iota_{n-1} p_{n}^1 + q_{n}^3) \\ =& \pi(q_n^3) \end{align*}

2.3. resolution

2.3.1. chain complex

follows as

\begin{align*} p_n^2 p_{n+1}^2 =& (\iota_{n-2} p_{n-1}^1 + q_{n-1}^3) (\iota_{n-1} p_{n}^1 + q_{n}^3) \\ =& p_{n-1} p_n + q_{n-1}^3 q_n^3 \\ =& 0 + 0 \\ =& 0 \end{align*}

2.3.2.

using Mitchell's embedding theorem we may assume that \(\mathcal{A} = \mathrm{RMod}\) for some ring \(R\).

Given

\begin{displaymath} \begin{tikzcd} P_3^1 \arrow[]{d}{} \arrow[rightarrowtail]{r}{} & P_3^2 \arrow[]{d}{} \arrow[twoheadrightarrow]{r}{}& P_3^3 \arrow[]{d}{} \\ P_2^1 \arrow[]{d}{} \arrow[rightarrowtail]{r}{} & P_2^2 \arrow[]{d}{} \arrow[twoheadrightarrow]{r}{}& P_2^3 \arrow[]{d}{} \\ P_1^1 \arrow[rightarrowtail]{r}{} & P_1^2 \arrow[twoheadrightarrow]{r}{}& P_1^3 \\ \end{tikzcd} \end{displaymath}

Let \(p_2^2 \in \mathrm{ker}(P_2^2 \twoheadrightarrow P_1^2)\). Then by commutativity of the square, \(\pi(p_2^2) \in \mathrm{ker}(P_2^3 \rightarrow P_1^3)\) and by exactness of the third column there exists a preimage \(p_3^3\). By surjectivity of \(P_3^2 \twoheadrightarrow P_3^3\) there exists a preimage \(p_3^2\). Then it follows that \(p_2^2 - \pi(p_3^2)\) is in the kernel of \(P_2^2 \rightarrow P_2^3\). Hence by exactness of the row, there exists a preimage \(p_2^1\). Furthermore \(p_2^2 - \pi(p_3^2)\) is still in the kernel of \(P_1^2 \rightarrow P_2^2\), as \(p_2^2\) is in the kernel by assumption and \(\pi(p_3^2)\) by exactness. Thus since \(P_1^1 \rightarrow P_1^2\) is injective it follows that \(p_2^1\) is in the Kernel of \(P_2^1 \rightarrow P_1^1\). Then by exactness there exists a preimage \(p_3^1\). At last \(\pi(p_3^1) + p_3^2 \in P_3^2\) is a preimage of \(p_2^2\) under \(P_3^2 \rightarrow P_2^2\), as

\begin{align*} \pi(\pi(p_3^1)) = \pi(p_2^1) = p_2^2 - \pi(p_3^2) \end{align*}

and thus \(\pi(p_3^2)\) cancels.
