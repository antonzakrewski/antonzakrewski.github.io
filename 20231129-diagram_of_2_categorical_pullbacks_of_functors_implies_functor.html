diagram of 2-categorical pullbacks of functors implies functor

1. Proposition

Let \(\mathcal{C}_0, \mathcal{C}_1, \mathcal{C}_2\) and \(\mathcal{D}_0, \mathcal{D}_1, \mathcal{D}_2\) be categories and

\begin{displaymath} \begin{tikzcd} \mathcal{C}_1 \arrow[]{r}{\mathcal{F}_1} \arrow[]{d}{\mathcal{L}_1} & \mathcal{C}_0 \arrow[]{d}{\mathcal{L}_0} & \mathcal{C}_2 \arrow[swap]{l}{\mathcal{F}_2} \arrow[]{d}{\mathcal{L}_2}\\ \mathcal{D}_1 \arrow[swap]{r}{\mathcal{G}_1}& \mathcal{D}_0 & \arrow[]{l}{\mathcal{G}_2}\mathcal{D}_2 \\ \end{tikzcd} \end{displaymath}

a commuting diagram

Then there exists a canonical functor for the 2-categorical pullback of a functor diagram

\begin{displaymath} \begin{tikzcd} \mathcal{C}_1 \times_{\mathcal{C}_0} \mathcal{C}_2 \rightarrow \mathcal{D}_1 \times_{\mathcal{D}_0} \mathcal{D}_2 \end{tikzcd} \end{displaymath} \begin{align*} \mathcal{L}: \mathcal{C}_1 \times_{\mathcal{C}_0} \mathcal{C}_2 \rightarrow& \mathcal{L}_1 \times_{\mathcal{L}_0} \mathcal{L}_2 \\ (X_1,X_2, \alpha) \mapsto& (\mathcal{L}_1(X_1), \mathcal{L}_2(X_2), \mathcal{L}_0(\alpha)) \\ (f_1,f_2) \mapsto& \left( \mathcal{L}_1(f_1), \mathcal{L}_2(f_2)\right) \\ % (f_1,f_2,f_0) \mapsto& \left( \mathcal{L}_1(f_1), \mathcal{L}_2(f_2), \mathcal{L}_0(f_0)\right) \end{align*}

2. Proof

2.1. welldefined

Let \((X_1,X_2,\alpha)\) and \((Y_1,Y_2,\beta)\) be objects

2.1.1. domain / codomain

Then by commutativity, \(\mathcal{G}_1(\mathcal{L}_1(X_1)) = \mathcal{L}_0(\mathcal{F}_1(X_1))\) and \(\mathcal{G}_2(\mathcal{L}_2(X_2)) = \mathcal{L}_0(\mathcal{F}_2(X_2))\) and thus

\begin{align*} \alpha: \mathcal{F}_1(X_1) \rightarrow& \mathcal{F}_2(X_2) \\ \mathcal{L}_0(\alpha): \mathcal{L}_1(\mathcal{F}_1(X_1)) \rightarrow& \mathcal{L}_2(\mathcal{F}_2(X_2)) \\ \mathcal{L}_0(\alpha): \mathcal{G}_1(\mathcal{L}_1(X_1)) \rightarrow& \mathcal{G}_2(\mathcal{L}_2(X_2)) \end{align*}

2.1.2. morphism

Let \((f_1,f_2): (X_1,X_2,\alpha) \rightarrow (Y_1,Y_2, \beta)\). Then

\begin{displaymath} \begin{tikzcd} \mathcal{F}_1(X_1) \arrow[swap]{d}{\mathcal{F}_1(f_1)}\arrow[]{r}{\alpha}& \mathcal{F}_2(X_2) \arrow[]{d}{\mathcal{F}_1(f_2 )} \\ \mathcal{F}_1(Y_1) \arrow[]{r}{\beta}& \mathcal{F}_2(Y_2) \\ \end{tikzcd} \end{displaymath}

commutes Thus, since applying \(\mathcal{L}_0\) preserves the diagram, we get

\begin{displaymath} \begin{tikzcd} \mathcal{L}_0(\mathcal{F}_1(X_1)) \arrow[swap]{d}{\mathcal{L}_0(\mathcal{F}_1(f_1))} \arrow[]{r}{\mathcal{L}_0(\alpha)}& \mathcal{L}_0(\mathcal{F}_2(X_2)) \arrow[]{d}{\mathcal{L}_0(\mathcal{F}_2(f_2))} \\ \mathcal{L}_0(\mathcal{F}_1(Y_1)) \arrow[]{r}{\mathcal{L}_0(\beta)}& \mathcal{L}_0(\mathcal{F}_2(Y_2)) \\ \end{tikzcd} \end{displaymath}

By commutativity of

\begin{displaymath} \begin{tikzcd} \mathcal{C}_1 \arrow[]{r}{\mathcal{F}_1} \arrow[]{d}{\mathcal{L}_1} & \mathcal{C}_0 \arrow[]{d}{\mathcal{L}_0} & \mathcal{C}_2 \arrow[swap]{l}{\mathcal{F}_2} \arrow[]{d}{\mathcal{L}_2}\\ \mathcal{D}_1 \arrow[swap]{r}{\mathcal{G}_1}& \mathcal{D}_0 & \arrow[]{l}{\mathcal{G}_2}\mathcal{D}_2 \\ \end{tikzcd} \end{displaymath}

we get

\begin{align*} \mathcal{L}_0 \circ \mathcal{F}_1 =& \mathcal{G}_1 \circ \mathcal{L}_1 \\ \mathcal{L}_0 \circ \mathcal{F}_2 =& \mathcal{G}_2 \circ \mathcal{L}_2 \end{align*}

and hence

\begin{displaymath} \begin{tikzcd} \mathcal{G}_1(\mathcal{L}_1(X_1)) \arrow[swap]{d}{\mathcal{G}_1(\mathcal{L}_1(f_1))} \arrow[]{r}{\mathcal{L}_0(\alpha)}& \mathcal{G}_2(\mathcal{L}_2(X_2)) \arrow[]{d}{\mathcal{G}_2(\mathcal{L}_2(f_2))} \\ \mathcal{G}_1(\mathcal{L}_1(Y_1)) \arrow[]{r}{\mathcal{L}_0(\beta)}& \mathcal{G}_2(\mathcal{L}_2(Y_2)) \\ \end{tikzcd} \end{displaymath}

commutes

2.2. identity

Let \((X_1,X_2, \alpha)\) be an object, then \((\mathrm{id}_{X_1}, \mathrm{id}_{X_2})\) is the identity morphism

\begin{displaymath} \begin{tikzcd} \mathcal{F}_1(X_1) \arrow[swap]{d}{\mathcal{F}_1(\mathrm{id}_{X_1} )}\arrow[]{r}{\alpha}& \mathcal{F}_2(X_2) \arrow[]{d}{\mathcal{F}_1(\mathrm{id}_{X_2} )} \\ \mathcal{F}_1(X_1) \arrow[]{r}{\alpha}& \mathcal{F}_2(X_2) \\ \end{tikzcd} \end{displaymath}

Then by construction we get

\begin{align*} \mathcal{L}(\mathrm{id}_{X_1} , \mathrm{id}_{X_2}) =& (\mathcal{L}_1(\mathrm{id}_{X_1}, \mathcal{L}_2(\mathrm{id}_{X_2}) \\ =& (\mathrm{id}_{\mathcal{L}_1(X_1)}, \mathrm{id}_{\mathcal{L}_2(X_2)}) \end{align*}

which is the identity for \(( \mathcal{L}_1(X_1), \mathcal{L}_2(X_2), \mathcal{L}_0(\alpha))\)

2.3. functorality

Let \(X \coloneqq (X_1,X_2,\alpha), Y \coloneqq (Y_1,Y_2,\beta), Z \coloneqq (Z_1,Z_2, \gamma)\) be objects and \((f_1,f_2): X \rightarrow Y\) and \((g_1,g_2): Y \rightarrow Z\) be morphisms. Then by construction,

\begin{align*} \mathcal{L}(g_1,g_2) \circ \mathcal{L}(f_1,f_2) =& (\mathcal{L}_1(g_1), \mathcal{L}_2(g_2)) \circ (\mathcal{L}_1(f_1), \mathcal{L}_2(f_2)) \\ =& (\mathcal{L}_1(g_1) \circ \mathcal{L}_1(f_1), \circ \mathcal{L}_2(g_2) \circ \mathcal{L}_2(f_2)) \\ =& (\mathcal{L}_1(g_1 \circ f_1), \circ \mathcal{L}_2(g_2 \circ f_2)) \\ =& \mathcal{L}(g_1 \circ f_1, g_2 \circ f_2) \end{align*}
