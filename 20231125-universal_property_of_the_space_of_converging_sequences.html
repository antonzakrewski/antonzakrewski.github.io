universal property of the converging sequence space

1. Proposition

Let \((X, \mathcal{T})\) be a topological space, \(\mathrm{Seq}\) the converging sequence space. Then a map

\begin{align*} f: \mathrm{Seq} \rightarrow& X \\ 0 \mapsto& x_0 \\ \frac{1}{n} \mapsto& x_n \\ \end{align*}

is continuous if and only if \(x_n \rightarrow x_0\).

2. Proof

2.1. continuous \(\implies\) converges

Let \(U \subseteq X\) be an open neighbourhood of \(x_0\). Then \(f^{-1}[U]\) is an open neighbourhood of \(0\), hence contains by construction almost all \(\frac{1}{n} \in \mathrm{Seq}\). Thus almost all \(x_n \in U\) or

\begin{align*} \lim_{n \to \infty}\left(f(\frac{1}{n})\right) x_0 \end{align*}

2.2. converges \(\implies\) continuous

Note that each point \(\frac{1}{n}\) is clopen. Hence for an open set \(U \subseteq X\) with \(x_0 \not\in U\), it follows that \(f^{-1}[U]\) is open.

Suppose \(x_n \rightarrow x_0\) converges, then for any neighbourhood \(U\) of \(x_0\), almost all \(x_n\) lie in there. Hence \(f^{-1}[U]\) contains almost all \(\frac{1}{n}\) and is thus a neighbourhood of \(0\).
